Keynote: Apple Containerization: Secure, Private Containers on macOS - Madhu Venugopal, Apple

Apple has introduced containerization on macOS, aiming for enhanced security and privacy without compromising performance. This is achieved by running Linux containers within lightweight virtual machines (microVMs), utilizing Apple's virtualization framework. Each container operates in its own isolated environment, with resources allocated only when needed, ensuring subsecond startup times. Security is bolstered by direct booting the Linux kernel with minimal configurations, reducing the attack surface and enhancing performance. The file system within these microVMs lacks common utilities and dynamic libraries typically found in full virtual machines, further minimizing potential vulnerabilities. Processes are compiled as static executables to avoid linking to unneeded libraries. Developers are encouraged to leverage this technology by using the APIs provided in containerization for building custom solutions or enhancing existing ones via plugins. Keynote: How One Line of Code Freed 30,000 CPU Cores: Deep-Diving Fluent Bit at Petabyte... F. Ponce

YouTube

OpenAI, leveraging open-source technology, employs Fluent Bit for processing logs at scale to support their advanced AI models like GPT5. The team faces immense telemetry data, necessitating efficient logging solutions. Fluent Bit's CPU usage was a significant concern due to its deployment on every Kubernetes node. Initially assuming Fluent Bit spent most time in expensive string processing within Lua, they used Perf for investigations. Surprisingly, they discovered fstat 64 consumed the most CPU time—35% of each Fluent Bit process's time. This function, utilized by both tail and i-notify plugins, was called frequently to determine log file sizes, which wasn't efficient given the infrequent writes in their environment. This optimization resulted in a 50% reduction in CPU load across heavily loaded Fluent Bit pods. The team is now working on upstream improvements to enable specifying minimum intervals for better performance without disabling i-notify. The talk emphasizes the benefits of open-source software in enabling organizations to concentrate on core business problems, leveraging pre-built scalability solutions. Frank Mancina, from Haroxy Technologies, discusses the limitations of current service meshes in simplifying modern connectivity within today's complex enterprise environments. He highlights that service meshes, while intended to address connectivity issues, often relocate complexity elsewhere due to the fractured nature of enterprises—comprising multiple clouds, clusters, on-premises data centers, and legacy applications. Mancina identifies two main service mesh architectures: sidecar and sidecarless. Sidecar architectures, though effective at a smaller scale, become overly complex and costly as they're scaled to large numbers of pods. Sidecarless architectures distribute complexity across various distributed components (layer four proxies per node, layer seven proxies per namespace or service account), but still fail in unifying hybrid clouds, managing north-south traffic, and supporting federated setups. The core issue, according to Mancina, lies not at the service level but rather at the network boundaries. Haroxy's Universal Mesh aims to tackle this by eliminating sprawling sidecar and proxy deployments. This approach consolidates ingress, mesh, and proxy into a unified, federated system powered by Haroxy’s robust reverse proxy experience—spanning 20+ years of proven performance and reliability. Mancina outlines Universal Mesh's three pillars: the control plane (Haroxy Fusion) communicating with APIs across multiple clusters; the data plane relying on Haroxy for north-south and east-west communication, enabling omni-directional support across Kubernetes clusters, clouds, container services, and on-premises infrastructure. The Universal Mesh leverages intelligent routing and policies without forcing architectural compromises, thanks to its understanding of both Kubernetes resources and infrastructure resources. It supports flexible deployment models, allowing gateways external or internal to Kubernetes for traditional vs. cloud-native workloads. This results in simpler connectivity, reduced overhead, and no extra latency on every pod, node, or cluster. In essence, Haroxy's Universal Mesh promises a paradigm shift in enterprise connectivity by addressing the root causes of complexity within service meshes – rather than just tactical problems within constrained boundaries – ultimately enabling organizations to overcome limitations imposed by current connectivity and focus on driving innovation.

In this keynote, Greg Kroah-Hartman, a prominent Linux kernel developer, addresses the EU's Cyber Resilience Act (CRA), explaining its implications for open-source developers and maintainers. The CRA is essentially a software inventory law, requiring listing all software components in devices or products to ensure they're safe, up-to-date, and vulnerability-free. This applies to manufacturers and integrators but not to individual hobbyists or non-commercial entities using open-source software in closed systems. Kroah-Hartman reassures developers that the CRA isn't a cause for fear, emphasizing that most open-source projects already comply with this law's requirements since they're involved in the creation of secure and updated software. For maintainers or stewards managing larger open-source projects, there are two primary responsibilities: 1. Maintain an up-to-date contact point for reporting security issues. 2. Proactively engage with the security fix process, even without directly fixing it themselves. The CRA is expected to have practical tools and resources supporting compliance, such as the reuse tool by FSFE (Fraunhofer Society for Open Source) that generates Software Bill of Materials (SBOM), and checklists provided by OSS Foundation (OSSF). Manufacturers will start implementing the law in June 2023, with CRA-related assessments becoming formal. By September 2027, the entire regulation applies to all software users, giving open-source contributors ample time to adapt. As standards development progresses for cybersecurity best practices, they'll remain voluntary at first. Kroah-Hartman encourages participation in these ongoing efforts and highlights resources like the CRA site, training courses, documentation, FAQs, and future materials from the EU. In essence, while the CRA introduces new requirements, open-source developers can rest assured that their current practices already align with many aspects of this law, making compliance manageable without major changes for most individuals in the community.

The Kubernetes network driver—K&D—has been built on top of DRA, allowing pods to request specific resources via resource claims. This setup ensures better hardware utilization, predictable behavior, and enhanced performance by expressing exactly what is needed in a declarative manner. A demo showcased the use of DRA for configuring interfaces with desired MTU sizes, demonstrating its power and user-friendliness. Lionel Jouin from Red Hat announced the donation of Kubernetes Network Driver (DNET) to the Cloud Native Foundation (CNCF), making it a true project under the Kubernetes umbrella. DNET is designed to coordinate with various hardware drivers, optimizing performance for different components like accelerators, CPUs, and storage systems in an interconnected ecosystem. The community-driven nature of this development ensures ongoing collaboration, feedback, and progress towards creating a more unified and efficient Kubernetes networking environment for the future.

The complexity of interconnected services necessitates consistent scaling across the entire ecosystem rather than individual components. Amazon's forecasting models incorporate external factors like cultural events, as seen in the 2022 FIFA World Cup coinciding with Black Friday in Brazil and Serbia, by analyzing potential behavioral impacts on traffic patterns. In traditional systems, logs are collected, processed, and stored, often requiring decryption for analysis, which exposes the data to potential exposure risks. This issue arises during both data at rest and in transit phases since plaintext is necessary for processing and analysis. Confidential computing addresses these concerns by providing hardware-based encryption using Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs) like Intel TDX or AMD SEV. These systems securely isolate the computational process, ensuring that sensitive data remains encrypted even when being used or processed. Confidential containers extend this concept to Kubernetes, placing a VM inside a confidential container, maintaining end-to-end encryption of data from application ports all the way to the observability pipeline and storage in Elasticsearch. Key benefits of implementing confidential observability include: 1. Enhanced security for data in use (processing and analysis), complementing traditional protections for data at rest and in transit. 2. Preventing unauthorized access, as only authorized entities with proper credentials can decrypt and view the data within secure boundaries set by a key management service. 3. End-to-end encryption for observability pipelines, ensuring that logs and telemetry data remain protected both during transmission and processing stages. This approach is particularly valuable for organizations dealing with sensitive information such as government entities, healthcare providers, financial institutions, and companies adhering to strict regulatory requirements where data privacy is paramount. Confidential observability helps fill the gap left by traditional methods in safeguarding data during its usage phase.
